## Why This Project Matters to Me

I am a first-generation college student from an immigrant family. I learned to write in English as a skill I built from scratch — without tutors, without writing camps, without any of the advantages that make polished writing come easy. Online creative communities were spaces where I could share work and get real feedback from real people. The idea that someone could flood those spaces with AI-generated content and pass it off as human is not abstract to me. It undermines the trust that makes those communities valuable in the first place.

Provenance Guard is not about policing creativity. It is about giving audiences the context they need and giving human creators a fair way to be recognized for their work. That is why I built the appeals workflow with real care — a false positive (calling a human's work AI-generated) is worse than a false negative on a creative platform, and the system should reflect that asymmetry.

## 1. Detection Signals

### Signal 1: LLM Semantic Analysis (Groq llama-3.3-70b-versatile)

**What it measures:** Whether the text reads as human-written or AI-generated based on semantic patterns, stylistic coherence, and the overall feel of the writing. The LLM evaluates holistically — it considers vocabulary choices, sentence construction patterns, the presence or absence of personal voice, and whether the text has the kind of minor imperfections and idiosyncratic choices that characterize genuine human writing.

**Why human and AI writing differ here:** AI-generated text tends toward a particular kind of confident, well-organized coherence that is actually a signal. Humans meander, contradict themselves slightly, use unusual word choices, and make structural decisions that reflect personality rather than optimization. An LLM trained on human writing has seen enough of both to distinguish the texture of each.

**Output format:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0, where 0.0 means "very likely human" and 1.0 means "very likely AI-generated." The prompt explicitly asks for this number plus a brief reasoning string.

**Prompt structure:**
```
Analyze the following text and determine whether it was written by a human or generated by AI.

Return ONLY a JSON object with exactly these keys:
- "score": a float from 0.0 (clearly human) to 1.0 (clearly AI-generated)
- "reasoning": one sentence explaining the most important signal you observed

Text to analyze:
[TEXT]
```

**What it misses:**
- Formal human writing (academic papers, legal documents, business reports) that naturally sounds structured and consistent can score high (AI-like) even though it is human
- AI text that has been deliberately humanized — adding typos, shortening sentences, injecting first-person voice — can score lower than it should
- Very short texts (under 50 words) give the model too little to work with and produce unreliable scores

**Blind spot summary:** This signal is semantic and holistic. It catches the overall feel but can be confused by human writing that is unusually polished or AI writing that has been humanized.

---

### Signal 2: Stylometric Heuristics (pure Python, no external libraries)

**What it measures:** Statistical structural properties of the text that differ systematically between human and AI writing, computed purely from the text without any LLM call.

**Specific metrics computed:**

1. **Sentence length variance** — AI text tends to have more uniform sentence lengths. Humans naturally vary between short punchy sentences and long complex ones. Measured as the coefficient of variation (standard deviation / mean) of sentence lengths in words. Low variance = more AI-like.

2. **Type-token ratio (TTR)** — vocabulary diversity. The ratio of unique words to total words. AI text often reuses a narrower vocabulary within a piece. Higher TTR = more diverse = more human-like.

3. **Punctuation density** — commas, semicolons, em-dashes, ellipses per 100 words. AI text tends toward cleaner, more predictable punctuation. Unusual or dense punctuation patterns suggest human writing.

4. **Average word length variance** — similar to sentence length variance but at the word level. AI text uses words of more consistent length. Humans mix short common words with longer specialized ones more unpredictably.

**Output format:** A float between 0.0 and 1.0, where 0.0 means "very likely human" (high variance, diverse vocabulary) and 1.0 means "very likely AI" (uniform, low variance). Each metric is individually normalized to 0–1 and averaged into a single signal score.

**What it misses:**
- Minimalist writing styles (Hemingway-like short sentences, limited vocabulary) genuinely look AI-like to this signal — the signal cannot distinguish artistic choice from AI generation
- Translated text often has lower variance because translation tends to regularize structure
- Very short texts (under 100 words) don't produce stable statistics — variance of 3 sentences is meaningless

**Blind spot summary:** This signal is structural and statistical. It catches patterns the LLM might miss (a humanized AI text might fool the LLM but still have uniform sentence lengths) but it is blind to content and context.

---

### How the Two Signals Are Combined

The two signals are genuinely independent — one is semantic, one is structural. Their combination is more reliable than either alone because they fail in different ways.

**Combination formula:**
```
combined_score = (llm_score * 0.65) + (stylometric_score * 0.35)
```

**Weighting rationale:** The LLM signal gets higher weight (0.65) because it captures a broader range of signals and is more reliable on longer texts. The stylometric signal (0.35) acts as a structural check — if a text fools the LLM but has AI-like uniformity, the combined score will still lean toward AI. If both signals agree strongly, the combined score reflects high confidence. If they disagree, the combined score lands in the uncertain middle range.

**Stretch — ensemble weighting (3 signals):** If a third signal is added (e.g. perplexity-based scoring), the weighting becomes 0.50 LLM + 0.25 stylometric + 0.25 perplexity, with the same calibration logic.

## 2. Uncertainty Representation

**What does a combined score of 0.5 mean?** The two signals disagreed, or both were uncertain. The system genuinely does not know. A 0.5 is not "probably AI" — it is "the evidence is ambiguous and the system cannot make a confident call."

**Score thresholds and their meaning:**

| Score range | Interpretation | Label category |
|---|---|---|
| 0.00 – 0.30 | Both signals lean human; high confidence | `likely_human` |
| 0.31 – 0.55 | Signals disagree or are weak; genuine uncertainty | `uncertain` |
| 0.56 – 0.79 | Both signals lean AI; moderate confidence | `likely_ai` |
| 0.80 – 1.00 | Both signals strongly agree it is AI; high confidence | `likely_ai` (high confidence) |

**Why the uncertain range is asymmetric (0.31–0.55 rather than 0.40–0.60):** A false positive (calling human work AI) is worse than a false negative on a creative platform. So the threshold for calling something "likely human" is generous (anything under 0.30 qualifies) while the threshold for calling something "likely AI" requires a stronger signal (must exceed 0.55). The uncertain zone is intentionally wide to reflect genuine ambiguity.

**Confidence score returned to the API consumer:**

The raw combined score (0.0–1.0) is returned as `confidence`. Additionally, the API returns a `confidence_label` field: `"high"` when the score is below 0.20 or above 0.80, `"medium"` when the score is between 0.20–0.30 or 0.65–0.80, and `"low"` when the score is between 0.31–0.64 (the uncertain middle).

**Validation approach:** Test with four deliberately chosen inputs:
1. Clearly AI-generated text (polished, generic, no personal voice) → expect score > 0.75
2. Clearly human text (casual, irregular, personal) → expect score < 0.25
3. Formal human writing (academic register) → expect score in 0.35–0.60 range, label = uncertain
4. Humanized AI text (added typos, shortened sentences) → expect score in 0.45–0.70 range

If inputs 1 and 2 do not produce scores in their expected ranges, the scoring is miscalibrated and needs adjustment before moving on.

## 3. Transparency Label Design

The label must communicate the result in plain language. A non-technical reader — a blogger, a poet, a fiction writer — should understand immediately what it means. No jargon: no "classifier output," no "logit score," no "confidence interval."

**Design principle:** The label should always acknowledge uncertainty honestly and always mention that the creator can appeal. A false positive is worse than a false negative; the label should reflect that by being conservative in its language about "AI-generated" claims.

---

### Label Variant 1: High-confidence AI (score ≥ 0.80)

```
⚠️ AI-generated content detected

Our analysis strongly suggests this content was created with AI assistance.
This does not prevent it from being shared, but it will be labeled for readers.

If you wrote this yourself, you can submit an appeal and we will review it.
Appeals are reviewed by a human within 48 hours.
```

---

### Label Variant 2: Uncertain (score 0.31–0.79)

```
ℹ️ Origin unclear

Our analysis couldn't confidently determine whether this content was
written by a person or created with AI assistance.

It will be shared with this notice attached. If you are the human author,
you can submit an appeal to have your work reviewed and the label updated.
```

---

### Label Variant 3: High-confidence human (score ≤ 0.30)

```
✅ Appears human-written

Our analysis suggests this content was written by a person.
No label will be shown to readers.

Note: This is an automated assessment and may not be fully accurate.
```

---

**What makes these labels different from each other:**
- Variant 1 uses "strongly suggests" and "detected" — active language signaling a clear finding
- Variant 2 uses "couldn't confidently determine" — explicitly admits uncertainty
- Variant 3 uses "appears" and "suggests" — hedged language even on the human verdict, reflecting that we are never 100% certain

Each variant differs in tone, in what it tells the reader, and in what it recommends the creator do. The difference is not just a number — it is a different communicative act.

## 4. Appeals Workflow

**Who can submit an appeal:** Any creator who has received a `content_id` from a prior `/submit` response. The content_id is the link between the submission and the appeal.

**What information the appeal captures:**
- `content_id` — links to the original submission
- `creator_reasoning` — free text from the creator explaining why they believe the classification is wrong. Examples: "I wrote this from personal experience as a non-native English speaker," "This is my academic writing style which tends to be more formal," "I can provide a draft history showing my revision process."
- `timestamp` of the appeal submission

**What the system does when an appeal is received:**
1. Looks up the original submission by `content_id` in the audit log
2. Updates the content's status from `"classified"` to `"under_review"`
3. Appends a new audit log entry with `entry_type: "appeal"`, the creator reasoning, and the timestamp
4. Returns a confirmation JSON: `{"status": "appeal_received", "content_id": "...", "message": "Your appeal has been received and will be reviewed within 48 hours."}`
5. Does NOT automatically re-classify — a human reviewer sees the original signal scores, the transparency label, and the creator's reasoning

**What a human reviewer sees when they open the appeal queue (GET /log filtered by status):**
- The original submission text
- Both individual signal scores (LLM and stylometric)
- The combined confidence score
- The transparency label that was applied
- The creator's reasoning text
- The timestamp of both the original submission and the appeal

**No automated re-classification:** The system explicitly does not auto-reclassify on appeal. This is intentional — automated re-classification based on creator input would be gameable. A human makes the final call.

## 5. Anticipated Edge Cases

### Edge case 1: Non-native English speaker writing in formal register

A first-generation immigrant or international student who writes in a formal, careful English style — because that is how they learned to write — may produce text that scores high on the AI signal. Their sentence lengths may be more uniform, their vocabulary may be more restricted, and their writing may lack the casual idioms and controlled imperfection that our stylometric signal associates with native human writers.

**How the system handles it:** The uncertain label range (0.31–0.79) is intentionally wide precisely for this case. The label for an uncertain score says "couldn't confidently determine" and always mentions the appeal. The creator can explain their background in the appeal reasoning field. This edge case is also why the false-positive threshold is generous — we require a score above 0.55 before saying "likely AI," not 0.50.

### Edge case 2: Very short text (under 100 words)

A haiku, a tweet-length post, or a short poem does not give either signal enough data to work with. The stylometric heuristics become statistically meaningless (variance of 3 sentences), and the LLM is making a judgment with very little evidence.

**How the system handles it:** Short texts (under 100 words) receive an automatic flag in the response: `"short_text_warning": true`. The confidence score is capped at 0.70 regardless of what the signals return — we cannot be "highly confident" about a 3-sentence text. The label will always be at most `uncertain` for short texts, never `high-confidence AI`.

### Edge case 3: Intentionally humanized AI output

Someone who generates AI text and then edits it — adding typos, shortening sentences, injecting first-person language — can potentially fool both signals. The LLM signal may be confused by the personal voice, and the stylometric signal may be confused by the added variance.

**How the system handles it:** This is a genuine limitation we acknowledge honestly. The system is designed to catch unedited or lightly edited AI text, not adversarial humanization. The README's known limitations section names this explicitly. We do not claim to catch all AI-generated content — we claim to provide a meaningful signal with appropriate uncertainty.

### Edge case 4: Collaborative human+AI writing

A creator who writes a first draft and then uses AI to polish it, or who uses AI to generate ideas and writes their own sentences, produces a hybrid that neither signal can cleanly classify. This is a genuine gray area that our binary framing (human vs. AI) does not handle well.

**How the system handles it:** The uncertain label is the honest answer for hybrid content. We do not force a binary verdict on genuinely mixed content. The label says "origin unclear" and offers the appeal path.

```
               content_id, message: "...reviewed within 48h"}

  - Original classification entry
  - Appeal entry with reasoning
  - Status: "under_review"
```

### Data storage

All submissions and appeals are stored in a single `audit_log.json` file as a list of structured entries. SQLite is an acceptable alternative but JSON is simpler for a single-server demo. The log is append-only — nothing is ever deleted, status is updated in-place on the matching entry.

## Rate Limiting

**Chosen limits:** `10 per minute; 100 per day` per IP address.

**Reasoning:**

A legitimate creator submitting their own work would rarely submit more than once or twice in a sitting. Ten per minute gives generous headroom for testing and development while still catching simple flooding scripts that fire requests as fast as possible. A real creative platform would have per-user auth instead of per-IP limiting, but per-IP is appropriate for a demo system.

100 per day per IP prevents a single actor from exhausting the Groq API credits by running automated submissions overnight. At the free tier, Groq has rate limits on its own side — our per-day limit adds a layer of protection before we even hit their limits.

**What a real platform would do differently:** Rate limit by authenticated user ID, not IP. Apply higher limits to verified creators and lower limits to anonymous readers. Add a separate higher limit for the /log and /appeal endpoints since those don't hit the LLM.

## Known Limitations

1. **Formal human writing is consistently misclassified.** Academic papers, legal writing, business reports, and other high-register human writing tend to score in the AI-likely range because they have the structural uniformity (consistent sentence lengths, controlled vocabulary, no casual asides) that both signals associate with AI. A first-gen student writing a formal essay may get flagged. The appeal workflow is the intended path for these cases, but the false positive rate for formal register is higher than for casual writing.

2. **Very short texts are unreliable.** Under 100 words, the stylometric metrics are statistically meaningless and the LLM has too little context. We cap confidence at 0.70 and add a warning, but the system should not be trusted on haikus or tweet-length posts.

3. **Adversarial humanization is not detected.** Someone who generates AI text and carefully edits it to add personal voice, irregular punctuation, and variable sentence lengths can fool both signals. The system is designed for the common case (unedited or lightly edited AI text), not adversarial evasion.

4. **No memory across submissions.** The system treats every submission independently. A creator with a known writing style cannot benefit from that history — every piece starts fresh. A real system would build creator-specific baseline profiles over time.
